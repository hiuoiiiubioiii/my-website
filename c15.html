Increment & Decrement Operators in C

Instructor: Jayanti Khatri Lamba | Comprehensive Visual Notes

1. Core Concepts

Increment (++) and Decrement (--) are unary operators. This means they only require one operand (like x) to function.

  • Increment (++): Increases the value of the operand by 1. Equivalent to x = x + 1.
  • Decrement (--): Decreases the value of the operand by 1. Equivalent to x = x - 1.

Instructor Note: While usually used with integers, you can use these with float values as well (e.g., 10.1++).

2. Prefix vs. Postfix Notation

These operators can be placed in two different positions relative to the variable. The position completely changes the order of execution in the computer's memory.

  • Prefix (Pre): Operator comes before the operand (++x). Action happens first.
  • Postfix (Post): Operator comes after the operand (x++). Action happens last (after the semicolon completes the statement).

7. Preview: Precedence & Associativity

What happens when you combine multiple operators in a single line? (e.g., ++a + a++ - ++a). The instructor notes two rules that will be covered in depth in the next video:

  • Precedence: Increment/Decrement operators have higher precedence than Assignment (=) and standard Arithmetic (+, -) operators. They get evaluated first.
  • Associativity: When multiple increment/decrement operators appear side by side, the compiler reads them from Right to Left.
