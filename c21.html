C Operator Precedence & Associativity

Complete visual study notes. Scroll down to master the concepts step-by-step.

1. The Core Problem

In a programming expression containing multiple operators, variables, and constants, the C compiler needs strict mathematical rules to know exactly which operation to perform first.

Visualizing the Dilemma

Look at this simple expression: 2 + 3 * 4. How should the computer read this?

❌ Case 1: Blindly reading Left to Right
2 + 3 * 4
↓
5 * 4 = 20 (Wrong!)
✅ Case 2: Using Precedence (Multiplication beats Addition)
2 + 3 * 4
↓
2 + 12 = 14 (Correct!)

2. The Two Golden Rules

To solve the problem above, the C compiler relies on two distinct properties of operators:

1. Operator Precedence (Priority)

Precedence decides which operator is executed first. Think of it like the BODMAS/PEMDAS rule in standard math. Multiplication has a higher "priority" than addition.

2. Operator Associativity (Direction)

Associativity decides the sequence of execution when two or more operators in an expression have the exact same precedence. It tells you whether to calculate from Left-to-Right (L → R) or Right-to-Left (R → L).

Example: Division and Multiplication share the exact same priority.

2 * 3 / 2 * 12 (Rule: Left to Right)
↓
6 / 2 * 12
↓
3 * 12
↓
36

3. The Ultimate Precedence Chart

This chart dictates the execution priority of every operator in C. Priority 1 is the highest (executed first), and Priority 15 is the lowest.

Priority Operator Symbol(s) Name / Type Associativity
1 () [] . -> ++ -- Parentheses, Array, Member selection, Postfix inc/dec L → R
2 ++ -- + - ! ~ * & sizeof (type) Prefix inc/dec, Unary +/-, Logical/Bitwise NOT, Pointers, Sizeof, Cast R → L
3 * / % Multiplication, Division, Modulo L → R
4 + - Addition, Subtraction (Binary) L → R
5 << >> Bitwise Shift (Left, Right) L → R
6 < <= > >= Relational (Less, Greater) L → R
7 == != Equality (Equal, Not Equal) L → R
8 & Bitwise AND L → R
9 ^ Bitwise XOR L → R
10 | Bitwise OR L → R
11 && Logical AND L → R
12 || Logical OR L → R
13 ?: Conditional (Ternary) R → L
14 = += -= *= ... Assignment Operators R → L
15 , Comma Operator L → R

💡 The Instructor's Secret Shortcut

Don't memorize the whole Associativity column! Notice a pattern? Almost every single operator in C evaluates Left-to-Right.

You only need to memorize the Three Right-to-Left (R → L) Exceptions:

  1. Unary Operators (Priority #2) - Careful: Prefix is R→L, but Postfix is L→R!
  2. Ternary Operator ?: (Priority #13)
  3. Assignment Operators = (Priority #14)

4. Step-by-Step Practice Problems

Let's trace through the instructor's exact whiteboard examples to see how the compiler reads them.

Example A: Same Precedence everywhere

Expression has *, %, and /. They all share Priority #3. We use the Associativity rule: Left to Right.

a = 3 * 4 % 5 / 2
↓
a = 12 % 5 / 2
↓
a = 2 / 2
↓
a = 1

Example B: Introducing Parentheses

Parentheses () have Priority #1. They override everything else. We jump straight to them.

a = 3 * (4 % 5) / 2
↓
a = 3 * 4 / 2
↓
a = 12 / 2
↓
a = 6

Example C: Mixed Precedence

* has a higher priority than +. We execute both multiplications first (Left-to-Right), then add them.

a = 3 * 4 + 5 * 6
↓
a = 12 + 5 * 6
↓
a = 12 + 30
↓
a = 42

Example D: Parentheses override Mixed Precedence

Even though + is usually lower priority than *, the brackets force us to evaluate the addition first.

a = 3 * (4 + 5) * 6
↓
a = 3 * 9 * 6
↓
a = 27 * 6
↓
a = 162
